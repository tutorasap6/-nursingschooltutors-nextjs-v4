### Background and Significance

**Direct Care Project Context**

The Direct Care Project spans multiple weeks, progressing through distinct phases:

1. **Phase 1 — Assessment and Diagnosis:** Conduct a windshield survey, community assessment using Healthy People 2030 objectives, and identification of a priority health problem affecting the target population.
2. **Phase 2 — Planning:** Develop a community health teaching plan using evidence-based interventions tailored to the population's cultural, linguistic, and health literacy needs.
3. **Phase 3 — Implementation:** Deliver a health education presentation to the target community group.
4. **Phase 4 — Evaluation:** Assess knowledge gained using pre- and post-test data, reflect on teaching effectiveness, and recommend sustainability strategies.

### Phase 1: Community Assessment and Health Diagnosis

**Windshield Survey Findings:**

A windshield survey was conducted in [community name] to assess environmental and social determinants of health. Key observations included:

- **Housing:** Mix of single-family homes and multi-unit apartment buildings; evidence of deferred maintenance in lower-income areas; approximately 15% of structures showed signs of overcrowding.
- **Transportation:** Limited public transit options; most residents rely on personal vehicles; no bicycle lanes observed.
- **Food Access:** Two grocery stores within 3-mile radius; 4 fast food establishments within 0.5 miles of the survey area; no farmers' markets observed.
- **Recreation:** One public park (2 miles away); limited safe walking infrastructure.
- **Healthcare Access:** One federally qualified health center (FQHC); no urgent care or pharmacy within walking distance for most residents.

**Priority Health Problem Identified:** Hypertension management in adults aged 35–64 years, based on:
1. County health data showing hypertension prevalence 28% above state average
2. High sodium fast food availability and limited access to fresh produce
3. Low physical activity infrastructure
4. Survey finding of health literacy barriers in Spanish-speaking community members

### Phase 2: Teaching Plan Development

**Evidence-Based Teaching Plan:**

**Population:** Adults aged 35–64 years with hypertension in [community name]
**Topic:** Hypertension Management: DASH Diet, Physical Activity, and Medication Adherence
**Setting:** Community center meeting room
**Duration:** 45 minutes
**Format:** Interactive presentation with hands-on activities

**Learning Objectives:**
1. Participants will identify 3 modifiable risk factors for hypertension after the presentation.
2. Participants will demonstrate how to read a nutrition label for sodium content.
3. Participants will list 2 community resources for physical activity support.

**Teaching Strategies:**
- **Demonstration:** Blood pressure measurement technique
- **Teach-back:** Participants explain DASH diet principles in their own words
- **Group discussion:** Barriers and facilitators to medication adherence
- **Handouts:** Bilingual (English/Spanish) DASH diet guide, low-sodium recipe cards, and community resource list

**Cultural Considerations:**
Cultural competence requires adapting all health education materials to the community's cultural food preferences, primary language, and health beliefs. Materials were translated into Spanish and validated by a native speaker. Traditional foods were incorporated into the DASH diet teaching.

**Theory Application:** The Health Belief Model (HBM) was selected as the theoretical framework for this health education intervention because it directly addresses the barriers most commonly cited by hypertensive patients in avoiding treatment:
- **Perceived susceptibility:** Many patients underestimate their personal risk because hypertension is often asymptomatic
- **Perceived severity:** Patients may not recognize the serious complications of uncontrolled hypertension
- **Perceived benefits:** Education reinforces the evidence that treatment prevents stroke, MI, and CKD
- **Perceived barriers:** Addressing cost, side effects, dietary restrictions, and complexity of regimen
- **Self-efficacy:** Building confidence in patients' ability to monitor and manage their blood pressure
