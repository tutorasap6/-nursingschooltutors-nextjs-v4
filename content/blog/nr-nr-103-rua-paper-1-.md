## Introduction

The social determinants of health (SDOH) are the conditions in which people are born, grow, live, work, and age that shape their health outcomes. According to the World Health Organization (2023), these structural factors account for 30–55% of health outcomes — far exceeding the impact of clinical care alone. This paper explores three key determinants of health — socioeconomic status, educational access, and neighborhood environment — and examines their influence on population health disparities in the United States.

---

## Determinant 1: Socioeconomic Status and Health Outcomes

Socioeconomic status (SES) is one of the most powerful predictors of health across populations. Individuals in the lowest income quintile experience mortality rates two to three times higher than those in the highest income quintile (Braveman et al., 2022). Several mechanisms link low SES to poor health:

**Food Insecurity:** Approximately 10.5% of U.S. households experience food insecurity, which is directly associated with higher rates of type 2 diabetes, hypertension, and obesity (USDA, 2022). Without adequate access to nutritious food, chronic disease risk escalates significantly.

**Healthcare Access:** Low-income individuals are disproportionately uninsured or underinsured. Delayed preventive care leads to diagnoses at advanced disease stages, when treatment is more complex, costly, and less effective. As nurses, we must recognize that patients who miss appointments, discontinue medications, or decline follow-up care may be navigating structural barriers rather than exhibiting non-adherence.

**Stress and Allostatic Load:** Chronic poverty creates sustained psychosocial stress, elevating cortisol, promoting inflammation, and accelerating biological aging — a phenomenon called "weathering." This contributes to higher rates of cardiovascular disease, depression, and premature mortality in low-SES populations (Geronimus et al., 2019).

---

## Determinant 2: Educational Access and Health Literacy

Education is both a direct and indirect determinant of health. Direct effects include the knowledge and skills necessary to navigate the healthcare system, understand medical instructions, and engage in preventive behaviors. Indirect effects include the economic opportunities that education enables, which in turn affect housing, nutrition, and healthcare access.

The National Center for Education Statistics (2019) reports that 54% of U.S. adults read below a 6th-grade level, with health literacy deficits most pronounced among elderly, low-income, and racially minoritized populations. Low health literacy is associated with:

- Higher rates of medication errors and adverse drug events
- Poorer management of chronic conditions (diabetes, asthma, heart failure)
- Higher rates of emergency department use and hospitalization
- Reduced utilization of preventive services (mammography, colonoscopy, vaccination)

As nurses, integrating health literacy assessment into every patient encounter is essential. The teach-back method — asking patients to explain instructions in their own words — is an evidence-based strategy that confirms comprehension rather than assuming it. The American Nurses Association (2021) includes health literacy promotion as a core nursing competency in the updated Nursing Scope and Standards of Practice.

---

## Determinant 3: Neighborhood and Built Environment

Where a person lives profoundly shapes their health. Neighborhood-level factors including housing quality, access to green space, proximity to grocery stores, transportation infrastructure, and environmental hazards all influence health behaviors and outcomes.

**Food Deserts:** In low-income urban areas, residents often live more than one mile from the nearest grocery store. Studies document that residents of food deserts consume significantly fewer fruits and vegetables and experience higher rates of diet-related chronic diseases than residents of food-rich neighborhoods (Walker et al., 2021).

**Environmental Exposures:** Low-income communities and communities of color bear a disproportionate burden of environmental hazards — including air pollution from highways and industrial facilities, water contamination (as seen in Flint, Michigan), and soil contamination from legacy industrial sites. These exposures contribute to higher rates of asthma, lead poisoning (which permanently impairs cognitive development), cardiovascular disease, and certain cancers.

**Physical Safety:** Neighborhoods characterized by high crime rates limit opportunities for outdoor physical activity. Fear of violence leads residents to avoid parks and sidewalks, contributing to physical inactivity and its health consequences.

---

## Nursing Implications and Role of Advocacy

Understanding SDOH requires nurses to expand their practice beyond the bedside. The AACN Essentials (2021) identify social determinants competency as foundational for all nursing education levels. In practice, this means:

1. **SDOH Screening:** Incorporating validated screening tools such as PRAPARE (Protocol for Responding to and Assessing Patients' Assets, Risks, and Experiences) into nursing assessments.
2. **Resource Navigation:** Connecting patients with community resources — food banks, housing assistance, Medicaid enrollment support, and transportation services.
3. **Interprofessional Collaboration:** Partnering with social workers, community health workers, and public health professionals.
4. **Policy Advocacy:** Nurses must advocate for structural changes that address the root causes of health inequity. The ANA's Code of Ethics (2015) obligates nurses to address the social determinants of health as a professional responsibility.

---

## Conclusion

The social determinants of health are not peripheral concerns for nursing practice — they are central to it. Socioeconomic status, educational access, and neighborhood environment shape the health trajectories of individuals and populations in ways that clinical interventions alone cannot address. Nurses who understand these forces are better equipped to provide holistic, patient-centered care that addresses not just symptoms but the underlying conditions that produce illness. Professional nursing practice demands engagement with SDOH through assessment, advocacy, and interprofessional action.
