## Part 1: Atherosclerosis and Coronary Artery Disease

### Pathophysiology

Atherosclerosis begins with ENDOTHELIAL INJURY — damage to the vascular endothelium from risk factors including hypertension (shear stress), hyperlipidemia, smoking (nicotine-mediated oxidative stress), hyperglycemia, and inflammatory cytokines. Once injured, the endothelium becomes activated and expresses adhesion molecules (VCAM-1, ICAM-1), recruiting circulating monocytes.

**Foam Cell Formation:** Monocytes penetrate the intima and differentiate into macrophages. Oxidized LDL (ox-LDL) enters the intima and is engulfed by macrophages (via scavenger receptors), transforming them into FOAM CELLS — lipid-laden macrophages that form the FATTY STREAK (earliest atherosclerotic lesion, visible in the aorta of teenagers).

**Fibrous Plaque Development:** Foam cells release cytokines (PDGF, TGF-beta) that stimulate smooth muscle cell migration from the media to the intima. These cells proliferate and synthesize collagen and matrix proteins, forming a FIBROUS CAP over the growing lipid core. Angiogenesis within the plaque supplies nutrients — but also creates fragile neovascularization that can rupture internally.

**Vulnerable Plaque and ACS:** Plaques with large lipid cores and thin fibrous caps are "vulnerable" to rupture. When a vulnerable plaque ruptures, the necrotic lipid core is exposed to circulating blood → platelet aggregation, thrombus formation, and potentially complete occlusion → ACUTE CORONARY SYNDROME (unstable angina, NSTEMI, or STEMI depending on degree of occlusion and collateral circulation).

### Clinical Manifestations of CAD by Severity

| Severity | Clinical Presentation | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| <50% stenosis | Asymptomatic | Adequate perfusion at rest and exercise |
| 50-70% stenosis | Stable angina on exertion | Fixed stenosis; demand-supply mismatch with exercise |
| >70% stenosis | Stable angina at lower workloads | Significantly limited reserve |
| Plaque rupture | Unstable angina / NSTEMI / STEMI | Thrombus partially or completely occludes vessel |

### ECG Localization of MI

| MI Location | Affected Leads | Occluded Artery |
|---|---|---|
| Anterior MI | V1-V4 | LAD (Left Anterior Descending) |
| Lateral MI | I, aVL, V5-V6 | LCx (Left Circumflex) |
| Inferior MI | II, III, aVF | RCA (Right Coronary Artery) — 85% of patients |
| Posterior MI | ST depression V1-V3; tall R in V1-V2 | RCA or LCx |

## Part 2: Heart Failure — Pathophysiology and Compensatory Mechanisms

### Definition and Classification

Heart failure (HF) is a clinical syndrome in which the heart cannot pump sufficient blood to meet the body's metabolic demands, OR can only do so at the expense of elevated filling pressures. The ACC/AHA classify HF by ejection fraction:

- **HFrEF (EF <40%):** Reduced ejection fraction; systolic dysfunction; dilated, weakened ventricle
- **HFpEF (EF ≥50%):** Preserved ejection fraction; diastolic dysfunction; stiff, non-compliant ventricle

### Compensatory Mechanisms

When cardiac output falls, three compensatory mechanisms activate:

**1. Frank-Starling Mechanism:** Increased venous return stretches ventricular myocardium → increased force of contraction → temporarily restored stroke volume. However, in a failing heart, the Frank-Starling curve is depressed — the ventricle is already maximally stretched (operating on the flat portion of the curve).

**2. Neurohormonal Activation:**
- **SNS:** Increased norepinephrine → tachycardia, vasoconstriction. Initially compensatory, but chronic SNS activation is cardiotoxic — promotes cardiomyocyte apoptosis, arrhythmias, and progressive remodeling.
- **RAAS:** Decreased renal perfusion → renin → Angiotensin II → aldosterone → Na/water retention → increased preload. Angiotensin II also causes cardiac and vascular fibrosis.
- **ADH:** Released in response to decreased CO → water retention → dilutional hyponatremia in advanced HF.

**3. Ventricular Remodeling:** Chronic volume and pressure overload → ventricular hypertrophy and dilation. Initially compensatory (decreases wall stress), but progressive remodeling leads to dysfunctional geometry, mitral regurgitation from annular dilation, and further depression of cardiac function.

### Clinical Manifestations

**Left-sided HF (LHF):** Pulmonary congestion from elevated pulmonary capillary wedge pressure
- Dyspnea on exertion → paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea → orthopnea → dyspnea at rest
- Bilateral crackles (rales) at lung bases
- S3 gallop (from rapid ventricular filling — most specific sign of HF)
- Cough (cardiac cough — pink, frothy sputum in severe cases)

**Right-sided HF (RHF):** Systemic venous congestion (often secondary to LHF)
- Jugular venous distension (JVD)
- Peripheral pitting edema (dependent, bilateral)
- Hepatomegaly, ascites (in severe/chronic cases)
- Weight gain from fluid retention

### Nursing Management Priorities (ADHF)

1. **Airway/Oxygenation:** High Fowler's position; supplemental O₂ to SpO₂ >95%; prepare for BiPAP if SpO₂ <90%
2. **Hemodynamic Monitoring:** Continuous cardiac monitoring; vitals Q15-30 minutes; strict I&O; daily weights (AM, same scale, before breakfast)
3. **Pharmacotherapy:** IV loop diuretics (furosemide) — monitor potassium; vasodilators (nitroglycerin) — monitor BP; inotropes (dobutamine) for cardiogenic shock
4. **Patient Education:** Daily weight monitoring (>2 lb gain/day = call provider); sodium restriction (<2g/day); medication adherence; recognizing decompensation symptoms

## Part 3: Atrial Fibrillation — Mechanisms and Management

### Pathophysiology

Atrial fibrillation (AF) is the most common sustained cardiac dysrhythmia, affecting approximately 6 million Americans. It results from CHAOTIC ELECTRICAL ACTIVITY in the atria, driven by multiple unstable re-entry circuits.

**Triggers:** Ectopic electrical foci — most commonly from the PULMONARY VEIN OSTIA — generate rapid, disorganized impulses that bombard the atria. Structural remodeling (atrial fibrosis, dilation) creates the substrate that sustains AF.

**AV Node Response:** The AV node receives hundreds of chaotic atrial impulses per minute but acts as a gatekeeper — allowing only some to pass to the ventricles. The result: IRREGULARLY IRREGULAR ventricular response. At rapid ventricular rates (>100 bpm), decreased diastolic filling time compromises stroke volume.

**Loss of Atrial Kick:** Normally, coordinated atrial contraction provides 15-30% of ventricular filling at end-diastole. AF eliminates this coordinated contraction. In patients with diastolic dysfunction or hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, loss of atrial kick can precipitate acute hemodynamic decompensation.

**Thromboembolic Risk:** AF causes blood stasis in the fibrillating left atrial appendage (LAA) → thrombus formation → risk of cardioembolic stroke. AF causes approximately 25% of all strokes. Risk is quantified by CHA₂DS₂-VASc score; anticoagulation indicated for score ≥2 (males) or ≥3 (females).

### ECG Characteristics of AF
- Absent P waves (replaced by chaotic fibrillatory baseline "f-waves")
- Irregularly irregular R-R intervals (no discernible pattern)
- Narrow QRS complexes (unless aberrant conduction or bundle branch block)
- Ventricular rate varies (often 110-160 bpm in uncontrolled AF)
