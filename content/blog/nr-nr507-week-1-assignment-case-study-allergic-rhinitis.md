## Patient Presentation

**Chief Complaint:** "My nose won't stop running and I've been sneezing all morning."

**Patient:** J.T., a 28-year-old female administrative assistant presenting to the family practice clinic in April with a 3-week history of nasal congestion, clear rhinorrhea, paroxysmal sneezing (particularly in the morning), and bilateral eye itching and tearing. She reports that symptoms began when she started spending time outdoors gardening. She has a personal history of eczema and a family history of asthma (mother) and hay fever (father).

**Vital Signs:**
- BP: 118/74 mmHg
- HR: 72 bpm
- RR: 16 breaths/min
- Temperature: 37.0°C
- SpO₂: 99% on room air

**Physical Examination:**
- **Eyes:** Bilateral conjunctival injection, periorbital edema, "allergic shiners" (dark circles from venous congestion), watery discharge
- **Nose:** Pale, boggy, bluish-tinged nasal mucosa bilaterally; clear watery rhinorrhea; nasal polyp not present; "allergic crease" (transverse nasal crease from habitual nose rubbing)
- **Throat:** Mild pharyngeal erythema; cobblestoning of posterior oropharynx
- **Lungs:** Clear to auscultation bilaterally, no wheeze
- **Skin:** Areas of atopic dermatitis on antecubital fossae bilaterally

## Pathophysiological Mechanism of Allergic Rhinitis

Allergic rhinitis is a Type I IgE-mediated hypersensitivity reaction involving two phases:

### Phase 1: Sensitization (First Exposure)

When J.T. was first exposed to tree pollen (likely in prior seasons), her antigen-presenting cells (dendritic cells) processed the allergen proteins and presented them to Th2 lymphocytes. Th2 cells released IL-4 and IL-13, which stimulated B lymphocytes to undergo IgE class switching. These pollen-specific IgE antibodies were then produced and bound to Fc epsilon RI receptors on MAST CELLS in the nasal mucosa and conjunctiva, and on circulating BASOPHILS. J.T. became sensitized — primed for reaction — with no symptoms during this phase.

### Phase 2: Immediate Hypersensitivity Response (Re-exposure)

Upon re-exposure to tree pollen this spring, pollen proteins cross-link the allergen-specific IgE antibodies on sensitized mast cells. This cross-linking triggers MAST CELL DEGRANULATION, releasing preformed mediators within minutes:

| Mediator | Effect | Clinical Manifestation |
|---|---|---|
| Histamine | Vasodilation, increased vascular permeability, pruritus | Rhinorrhea, nasal congestion, itching, sneezing |
| Leukotrienes (LTC4, LTD4) | Prolonged bronchoconstriction, mucus hypersecretion | Nasal congestion (late phase), potential wheeze |
| Prostaglandin D2 | Vasodilation, mucus secretion | Congestion, rhinorrhea |
| Tryptase | Tissue remodeling | Marker of mast cell activation |

### Late-Phase Response (4–8 Hours After Exposure)

Cytokines released during the early phase (IL-5, eotaxin) recruit EOSINOPHILS to the nasal mucosa. Eosinophil degranulation releases major basic protein (MBP) and eosinophilic cationic protein (ECP), causing epithelial damage and sustained inflammation. This late-phase response explains why J.T.'s nasal congestion persists even hours after initial allergen exposure.

**Why does J.T. have pale, boggy nasal mucosa (not red)?** Unlike infectious rhinitis (which causes erythematous mucosa from vasodilation in acute infection), allergic rhinitis causes VENOUS ENGORGEMENT of the nasal turbinates from histamine-mediated increased vascular permeability and blood pooling → bluish, pale, boggy appearance.

## Differential Diagnosis

| Diagnosis | Supporting Features | Against |
|---|---|---|
| Allergic rhinitis (primary) | Seasonal onset, clear rhinorrhea, bilateral itchy eyes, family history atopy, personal atopy, seasonal timing (spring) | — |
| Infectious (viral) rhinitis | — | No fever, no purulent discharge, >3 weeks duration (viral URIs typically resolve in 7-10 days), itching prominent |
| Non-allergic rhinitis | Perennial symptoms | Seasonal onset, IgE-mediated features |
| Sinusitis | — | No facial pain/pressure, no purulent discharge, fever absent, CT not indicated |

**Primary Diagnosis: Seasonal Allergic Rhinitis (ICD-10: J30.1 — Allergic rhinitis due to pollen)**

## Evidence-Based Treatment Plan

### Pharmacological Management (Per ARIA 2022 Guidelines)

**Step 1 — First-line: Intranasal Corticosteroids (INCs)**

Fluticasone propionate (Flonase) 50 mcg/spray, 2 sprays each nostril once daily

*Mechanism:* INCs reduce nasal inflammation by suppressing multiple inflammatory mediators (histamine, leukotrienes, cytokines, prostaglandins) at the mucosal level. They are the most effective single agent for moderate-to-severe allergic rhinitis.

*Patient Teaching:*
- Prime pump before first use (6-7 actuations)
- Tilt head slightly forward and aim away from nasal septum (toward the outer wall)
- Sniff gently — do NOT snort forcefully
- Onset: 1-2 weeks for full effect; consistent daily use required
- Shake before each use

**Step 2 — Add: Second-generation oral antihistamine for breakthrough symptoms**

Cetirizine (Zyrtec) 10mg once daily OR Loratadine (Claritin) 10mg once daily (preferred: cetirizine for faster onset; loratadine if sedation is a concern)

*Mechanism:* Competitive H1-receptor antagonists. Second-generation antihistamines have minimal CNS penetration (less sedation). Effective for rhinorrhea, sneezing, and ocular itching.

**Step 3 — For ocular symptoms:**
Olopatadine hydrochloride (Pataday) ophthalmic solution, 1 drop affected eye(s) once daily

---

### Non-Pharmacological Management

1. **Allergen Avoidance:**
   - Monitor local pollen counts (available at pollen.com or weather apps); limit outdoor activities when counts are high (>500 grains/m³)
   - Keep windows closed during peak pollen season; use air conditioning
   - Shower and change clothes after outdoor exposure
   - Wear wraparound sunglasses outdoors

2. **Nasal Irrigation:**
   - Saline nasal rinse (NeilMed or Neti pot) twice daily removes allergens and mucus
   - Use distilled or previously boiled water only

3. **Allergen Immunotherapy (Long-term consideration):**
   - Referral to allergist for skin prick testing to identify specific allergens
   - Subcutaneous immunotherapy (SCIT) — 3-5 year treatment course, 85% reduction in symptom severity
   - Sublingual immunotherapy (SLIT) — available for grass pollen, effective alternative for needle-averse patients

## Patient Education

The patient was educated on:
- The difference between allergic and infectious rhinitis
- How to correctly use intranasal corticosteroid spray (technique demonstrated and return-demonstrated)
- The importance of consistent daily INC use — effectiveness is maximal with regular use, not PRN
- Signs requiring follow-up: new-onset wheeze, dyspnea, purulent nasal discharge, fever, or severe eye pain

---

## Follow-Up

Return visit in 4-6 weeks to assess treatment response. If symptoms persist despite optimal pharmacotherapy, referral to allergist for skin prick testing and immunotherapy evaluation.
