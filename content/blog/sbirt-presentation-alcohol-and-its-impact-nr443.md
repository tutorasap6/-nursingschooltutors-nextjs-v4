## What is SBIRT?

**SBIRT (Screening, Brief Intervention, and Referral to Treatment)** is a comprehensive, evidence-based public health framework for addressing problematic alcohol and substance use in healthcare and community settings.

**Three Components:**
1. **Screening** — Rapid validated assessment of alcohol use risk level
2. **Brief Intervention (BI)** — 5–20 minute motivational conversation for risky (non-dependent) use
3. **Referral to Treatment (RT)** — Structured connection to care for Alcohol Use Disorder (AUD)

**Evidence Base:** Cochrane systematic review (O'Connor et al., 2018): Brief interventions reduce weekly alcohol consumption by an average of 38 grams (~3 standard drinks) over 12 months in primary care settings.

---

## Epidemiology of Alcohol Use

According to the National Survey on Drug Use and Health (SAMHSA, 2022):
- **69.5%** of U.S. adults reported past-year alcohol use
- **25.8%** reported binge drinking in the past month (≥4 drinks women; ≥5 drinks men within ~2 hours)
- **28.6 million Americans** (10.2%) had Alcohol Use Disorder in 2021
- Only **7.6%** of those with AUD received any treatment

**Economic Impact:** $249 billion annually (CDC, 2022) — workplace productivity loss (72%), healthcare costs (11%), criminal justice (10%).

## Health Consequences of Problematic Alcohol Use

**Physiological:**
- Liver: Fatty liver → alcoholic hepatitis → cirrhosis → hepatocellular carcinoma
- Cardiovascular: Cardiomyopathy, atrial fibrillation, hypertension
- Neurological: Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome (thiamine deficiency), peripheral neuropathy
- Cancer: Oral cavity, pharynx, esophagus, liver, colorectal, breast
- Pregnancy: Fetal Alcohol Spectrum Disorders (FASDs) — leading preventable intellectual disability cause

**Social:**
- Intimate partner violence, child neglect, driving impairment (DUI), occupational dysfunction
- Bidirectional relationship with depression, anxiety, PTSD

## Screening Tools

### AUDIT-C (3 Questions — Validated for Primary Care)

**Q1:** How often do you have a drink containing alcohol?
(0=Never → 4=4+ times/week)

**Q2:** How many drinks do you have on a typical drinking day?
(0=1-2 → 4=10+)

**Q3:** How often do you have 6 or more drinks on one occasion?
(0=Never → 4=Daily/almost daily)

**Scoring:** Women ≥3 positive; Men ≥4 positive
**Sensitivity:** 86% | **Specificity:** 89%

### CAGE Questionnaire (Brief 4-Question Screen)
- **C** — Cut down: "Have you ever felt you should cut down on drinking?"
- **A** — Annoyed: "Have people annoyed you by criticizing your drinking?"
- **G** — Guilty: "Have you ever felt guilty about drinking?"
- **E** — Eye-opener: "Have you ever had a drink first thing in the morning?"

**Score ≥2** warrants AUD assessment.

## Brief Intervention: FRAMES Model

**F — Feedback:** Share personalized results
*"Your score indicates drinking above safe levels, increasing your risk for liver disease and hypertension."*

**R — Responsibility:** Emphasize autonomy
*"This is your decision — only you can make this change."*

**A — Advice:** Clear, non-judgmental recommendation
*"I recommend reducing to no more than 7 drinks/week for women, with no more than 3 on any day."*

**M — Menu:** Offer options
*"Many people find tracking drinks helpful, or scheduling alcohol-free days. What might work for you?"*

**E — Empathy:** Reflective listening
*"It sounds like drinking has become a way to manage stress. That makes a lot of sense given what you're dealing with."*

**S — Self-Efficacy:** Build confidence
*"You've made changes before — you can do this."*

## NIAAA Low-Risk Drinking Guidelines

**Women:** ≤3 drinks/day; ≤7 drinks/week  
**Men:** ≤4 drinks/day; ≤14 drinks/week  
**Anyone:** No alcohol during pregnancy, with certain medications, or when driving.

## Community Health Nursing Role

**Assessment Settings:**
- Prenatal visits (screen all at first visit — no safe level in pregnancy)
- School-based health centers (adolescent prevention)
- Home visits for postpartum, elderly, chronically ill
- Community health fairs, mobile health units

**Social Determinants:**
- Alcohol outlet density strongly correlates with community-level harm
- Poverty and unemployment increase risk
- Social norms and peer influence are modifiable targets

**Community Interventions:**
1. Implement SBIRT at community health fairs — train CHWs in AUDIT-C administration
2. Prenatal education: Emphasize no safe level during pregnancy; FASDs are 100% preventable
3. Adolescent prevention: Evidence-based programs addressing social norms
4. Policy advocacy: Minimum unit pricing; regulation of outlet density

**Referral for AUD — Pharmacotherapy Options:**
- **Naltrexone:** Reduces craving/rewarding effects (opioid receptor antagonist)
- **Acamprosate:** Reduces withdrawal-related craving in abstinent patients
- **Disulfiram:** Aversion therapy (acetaldehyde accumulation with alcohol)

**Behavioral Treatment Referrals:**
- Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy (CBT) for AUD
- Twelve-Step programs (Alcoholics Anonymous)
- SMART Recovery (secular, evidence-based peer support)

## Case Application: Maria

**Patient:** Maria, 38-year-old Latina woman | AUDIT-C score: 5 (positive screen)
Drinks 2–3 glasses wine nightly; primary caregiver for disabled mother.

**Brief Intervention Response:**
- *Feedback:* "Your score is above safe levels for women — this level of drinking increases liver disease and cancer risk."
- *Empathy:* "Caregiving 24/7 is exhausting. It makes sense wine feels like relief."
- *Advice:* "I recommend 1 glass most nights with 2 alcohol-free days per week."
- *Menu:* "Would sparkling water help as a replacement ritual, or calling a friend?"
- *Follow-up:* NIAAA Spanish brochure; phone check-in in 4 weeks
